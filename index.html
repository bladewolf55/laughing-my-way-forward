Fail Me Once, Shame On You

2023-03-06 19:51

What do you do when the person who scheduled your phone interview doesn't call you for a second time?

Well, what I did after fifteen minutes was turn off my headset, resolve to ignore any calls, and applied for the same position at another franchise location.

What will I do if the person contacts me, apologizes, asks to try again? Probably tell the truth.

"You didn't show up for two interviews. That tells me I shouldn't work with you, no matter what the reasons."

Fail me twice, shame on you twice as much and shame on me if I give you another chance. This is, after all, not a marriage or my child. It's a low-paying job to get my foot in the door, and it's not the only door in town.

I also applied to a different job teaching fitness classes for seniors. Since I'm a senior, that's probably a good fit.

On the home front, my wife and I played Bananagrams tonight. I told her and myself and the Cosmos that I didn't expect to win, I wouldn't win, and I didn't win. Her vocabulary and experience at words games is just too much.

It's a good game, but not for us, not for now, not in the "it makes me laugh" kind of way I want to be playing.

We're still on the hunt.

This is my advice. Find a game you enjoy playing with your spouse who is also one of your best friends. Not a game you enjoy winning, but playing. Whatever that means to both of you.

Dochi? You Know It!

2023-03-05 18:54

This'll be a short entry. I didn't sleep so well last night, not due to depression but instead my belly aching--and not the slang kind.

It's a chronic thing I won't dwell on in this post. All was soothed when my wife took me out for dochi.


Another Believer, CC BY-SA 4.0 https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

She had salted caramel. I had a matcha and a cinnamon crunch.

Was it world shattering, the microsecond man-made, more-energy-out-than-in fusion reaction that proves the sun isn't the only cosmic energy game in town?

I wouldn't go that far. It was an iced donut with a slightly chewy coating that was somehow satisfying. On the other hand, unlike nuclear fusion, I could hold it in my hand and feed bits to my wife. The latter drew an approving smile from the father of a family who were clearly there to pick up a big box of the things. At one point I wasn't sure if their filled box would survive the thirteen feet to the exit without being emptied. (More by parents than kids, I will not comment.)

The dochi were delightful. We didn't set out for downtown this morning in search of euphoria. Just a modicum of happy, a slice of grinny, a palate-pleasing, portmanteau pastry.

It worked. And the iced Vietnamese coffee wasn't bad, either. Cảm ơn!

Don't Forget to Expect a Slingshot

2023-03-04 20:16

Yesterday was a bad day, last night was a very bad night, and this morning wasn't too swell, either.

It's one thing to give up a 30 year career and feel like the possibilities are endless. It's another to feel the pain of, essentially, the death of part of yourself. And then ya gotta expect that your wife might have her own feelings on the subject and that those feelings may not be all daffodils and triple ice cream banana splits.


By Edward Allen Lim - https://www.flickr.com/photos/kwein_01/3875309388/, CC BY 2.0, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=70288050

This isn't a transcript of how the conversation went in reality. It is how it went in my heart.

"Are you disappointed in me?"

"For quitting your job without anything lined up, jeopardizing our retirement, pulling the rug out from under me, and surfacing a solid chunk of my childhood angst? 'Disappointed' might not be the ACT vocab word you're looking for."

"This isn't going to be a short talk, is it?"

"You might call that unlikely, slick."

The weather was cloudy, rainy, and cold. I'm one of those people whose emotions are lock-stepped to the weather like hormonal teens at a square dance. It's a big reason we moved to one of the sunniest states in the union. This is a real condition, my brain gets foggy, it's hard to concentrate, and the chances of depression in the forecast are in in the upper 80s. I was already, in a word, down.

What destroyed me wasn't the talk. It was lying in bed, dwelling and reviewing and reliving and--finally--trying to think of any job in my life that a) I'd enjoyed and b) had not included a fight with management or open hostility with a coworker. I could think of two.

I've had about twenty jobs. Which sounds absurd, but I was a contract software developer for over a decade so that meant several different clients. Before that: food service, retail, office work. I quit a lot of them, often because I had a tiff with management. I grew up thinking work was a place where you didn't get along with others, especially your bosses, who were likely idiots. It wasn't on purpose, it's just what my kid brain got taught.

So, it's certainly not funny when you're lying there realizing the possibilities are certainly not endless, they are constrained by a terribly serious question: what job makes money and doesn't require me working with people?

(That's the extreme pessimistic, depressed version of the question. But you get the idea.)

Software development used to be that for me, because I was able to work somewhat independently. Then I bought a house, got married (second and final time) and decided I wanted stable employment. Seemed like a good idea at the time.

There's some pretty good research showing that people's sense of well-being is strongly influenced by their sense of purpose. After a steadily worsening experience at my steady job, I announced to my wife that I wanted to find a workplace where I believed in the mission, enjoyed the culture, and wrote good code. How hard could that be to find? Almost ten years and three companies later, the answer was: not happening.

Why? Well, go back to my first post: about half of what I don't like in software engineering is inherent to the field. And I can't just "suck it up."

So I broke. I lay in bed crying and heaving and my wife tried to comfort me at the same time I knew she was pissed which made me feel worse. Somehow I got to sleep. Somehow I got out of bed this morning, made my way to the local coffee shop, journaled (as the cool kids call it these days) for a couple of hours, then somehow came home to have another long talk.

Thankfully, it was a sunny morning, my wife was seeking understanding, we both brought our hard-learned marriage therapy skills1 to bear, leading to an emotionally exhausting and loving resolution.

We even resolved that it was OK for me to regularly tell her corny jokes. Like this one.

The rest of the day has gone well. I got out and spent some time with friends, playing the French game Pétanque. My wife and I grocery shopped together. Our dog checked in with us to make sure everything was back to normal and that we'd still both be feeding him.

It was a bad day, and a bad night, and a bad morning. But now it's OK. And that's what I need to remember.

  1. Gottman-based research and techniques. I highly recommend this. It's solid science and has a clinically demonstrated high success rate.↩
